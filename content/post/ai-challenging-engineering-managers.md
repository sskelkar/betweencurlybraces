The advent of agentic coding has had profound implications for software engineering and triggered a developer [identity crisis](https://boristane.com/blog/the-software-development-lifecycle-is-dead/).  While much of the attention has been focussed on the impact of AI on software engineers, it is worth dwelling into how it is impacting managers.


# TBD: People management

While the productivity gains brought by AI are phenomenal. For your average software engineer this is also a time of great apprehension and anxiety. With all the news about AI-driven layoffs, engineers are understandably worried about their future prospects. As such, line mangers need to be prepared to have some difficult conversations in their 1:1 s that should be handled delicately.

While AI is empowering software engineers, it also increases expectations from everyone. If AI or any technology helps people deliver value faster, it unfortunately seldom means that they get more spare time. From organization's point of view, the expectation would be to deliver more value. That's just Parkinson's law.

What this means is that career framework or role expectations are going to be changed.

As managers, you might also see uneven AI adoption within your teams, which possibly creates performance differential among your engineers. As such you have to manage expectations in advance, and subtly communicate to the folks who are falling behind because they are not leveraging AI, so that it doesn't come as a surprise in annual performance reviews.

The boundaries of developer experience level are also blurring. You can have junior engineers who adopt faster and senior engineer who are slow to adopt. Which can impact team dynamics.

On a human level, as the industry goes through unprecedentedly fast changes, it becomes our job to communicate the changing expectations to our engineers.

Regarding layoffs - we don't know what the future looks like. But this is where we can play an important impact on the careers of our engineers by nudging them towards upskilling, AI adoption, high level thinking (directors), system thinking etc.

Empathy

In a well documented project, where there's less reliance on experienced knowledge, junior developers can be just as effective as senior engineers. This redefines the roles based on experience level and changes the stacking order for managers.

# TBD: Quarterly roadmaps are collapsing

As managers, developer time is one of our most precious resource, and we want to spend it on the most valuable thing that can provide compounding benefits. Quarterly roadmaps have been a standard practice to deciding the work that will provide the most value to the company.

The way it worked was, you planned the work for a quarter based on the estimated team capacity. Once you finalized the quarter, your job was to ensure that the deliverables remain on track. You would push back on any adhoc requests, if they didn't meet a certain priority threshold.

Importantly, you got a couple of months to decide the plan for the next quarter, as the previous quarter ran its course. The long term vision, planning, prioritization and execution followed a steady flow.

With the rise of Agentic coding, this flow has been severely disrupted. What took weeks to build, is now being done in hours or days. The available capacity is a shifting metrics that increases everyday as the team integrates AI adoption.

But is execution time is getting shorter, or puts pressure on vision to keep up.

It is already happening that you are able to accommodate that you couldn’t before.

You can say that this is just being Agile. But the turnaround time is just too quick

But another way to look at this is: if code is cheap to produce, it means strategic mistakes are less expensive than before. Opportunity cost is has gotten lower.

sunk cost fallacy no more

It helps to have some general guiding principles relevant to your team's situation, that sets your team on the right course. So even if there's not enough time to 'strategize', as long as the work you pickup that aligns with your guiding principles, you'll navigate your team in the right direction.

# TBD: Integrating AI in daily workflow / Adopting as SDLC is redefined


SDLC is going through yet another major change. High amount of AI-assisted code puts pressure on review and deployment processes. EMs have to keep an eye on this, so as team's delivery is not blocked.

Though you might have been used to traditional software engineering practices, EMs have to be open to adopting to the changing conditions. Let go of processes that don't make sense. You will need to create more practices.

Basically this is where you have to do a lot of system thinking to analyze what are the challenges and bottlenecks that are impacting your team's ability to delivery, and what you can do about them.

EMs are the first in line to promote adoption and trigger change in mindset.

Managers are responsible for delivery, but also accountable for incidents. So we need to balance the AI productivity gains against ai generated bugs to get the best risk adjusted returns,

Experiment with sprint duration. In my team we shifted from 2 to 3 week sprints

managers must facilitate proper training sessions for developers to learn AI usage. Don't assume if they are doing weekend projects, all will be fine on your critical production service.
