Agentic coding is transforming how software engineering is done. AI agents can help you write code faster, debug more efficiently, and ship features in a fraction of the time. This sounds like a dream come true—until you realize what it actually means.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: when you become more productive, you don't get to work less. You're expected to deliver more.

This isn't unique to software engineering. It's Parkinson's Law playing out: efficiency doesn't reduce working hours, it just creates more work. The factory worker who could make 10 widgets a day didn't get to go home early when machines helped them make 100 widgets a day. They were expected to make 100 widgets.

With great power comes great responsibility—and greater deliverables. AI gives you superpowers, but the bar rises with them. If you can now build in a week what used to take a month, you won't be working a quarter as much. You'll be expected to build four times as much.

So the question isn't whether AI will make your job easier. It's whether you can adjust your mindset to handle the new expectations that come with increased productivity.

But this situation is not completely unprecedented. There's a whole organization structure that was always in this situation - management.

AI agents are writing code with increasing autonomy. For example, Claude Agent Teams provide you a way of multiple agents actively working together to produce a code, so kind of working like a team. So if an engineer has team of AI agents writing code, perhaps they should try to think like Directors of Engineering.

Engineers need to zoom out and solve "system problems" (donella meadows)
